Regular Expressions

080915

Regular expressions (regex/regexp) are used to look for a specific set of patterns in strings.

They are basically a sequence of characters that allow you to look through and pick out the characters in the string that match that specific sequence.

When looking at regular expressions, there are two types of characters:

A metacharacter, carries a special meaning.

A regular character, which just carries its literal meaning.

With both types of characters combined, a pattern can be specified.

There are certain operations that allow us to create regular expressions:

The"or" operator is represented by a vertical bar |

For instance, if you wanted to find the word mum and mom, it would look like mum|mom

To group things together, parentheses can be used. They allow us to define the scope and the order we want the operators to be in. For example, mum|mom can also be written m(u|o)m.

A question mark ? after a character tells us how often that character can occur. For example, if we were to write colou?r, the ? mark after the character 'u' shows that there can be zero or one of the character 'u'. This would give us both "color" and "colour".

An asterisk * tells us that there is zero or more of the character that precedes it. For example, if we did jenn*y, it could give "jeny", jenny", "jennny", etc.

A plus sign + tells us there is one or more of the character that precedes it. Again, if we used this on jenn+y, it could give us "jenny", "jennny"," jennnny", but not "jeny".

And of course, we can combine these operations if you wanted to locate a very complex expression.

Regular expressions can look intimidating and confusing at first, but with a little guidance and practice, it can be a very useful tool!
